# VITALSPAN ART BIBLE

Version 1.0 — Permanent Visual Constitution

Vitalspan’s visual identity is called **The Living Still**: quiet, hand-painted editorial worlds in which scientific ideas appear as material transformations.

A Vitalspan cover does not illustrate a paper literally. It translates the issue’s central scientific tension into one restrained, memorable image.

The subject changes every week. The hand, light, restraint, and intelligence never do.

---

# I. Visual Constitution

## Article 1 — Vitalspan depicts inquiry, not wellness

Vitalspan is an editorial longevity magazine. Its images must suggest observation, ambiguity, time, adaptation, and consequence.

They must never resemble:

- A health-tracking product
- A hospital campaign
- A supplement advertisement
- A fitness brand
- A biotechnology pitch deck
- An AI-generated “future of medicine” image

## Article 2 — One cover, one idea

Every cover expresses one editorial proposition.

It may contain several objects, but they must form one visual sentence. A cover must remain understandable as a silhouette at thumbnail size.

## Article 3 — Metaphor over literalism

Scientific subjects are translated into physical relationships:

- Tension
- Accumulation
- Exchange
- Adaptation
- Thresholds
- Repair
- Constraint
- Divergence
- Interdependence
- Incomplete knowledge

Literal medical illustration is reserved for explanatory diagrams, never issue covers.

## Article 4 — Beauty must not overstate evidence

The image must preserve the confidence of the editorial thesis.

- High confidence may use a clear, stable relationship.
- Medium confidence should contain visible tension, incompleteness, or qualification.
- Low confidence must not invent unity. It should present several distinct signals held together compositionally, not scientifically.

A visually resolved transformation must never imply a mechanism, cure, or causal relationship unsupported by the issue.

## Article 5 — Restraint is the premium signal

Vitalspan does not compete for attention through spectacle.

The cover earns attention through:

- A surprising but intelligible metaphor
- Exceptional composition
- Material beauty
- Controlled color
- Quiet psychological tension
- Space for the reader to complete the thought

## Article 6 — The image contains no language

Generated artwork must contain no:

- Masthead
- Headline
- Labels
- Numbers
- Scientific notation
- Charts
- Watermarks
- Pseudo-lettering

Typography belongs to the editorial layout layer.

## Article 7 — Human dignity is non-negotiable

Aging is shown as depth, adaptation, patina, knowledge, or transformation—never deterioration for shock value.

People are never portrayed as diagnoses, before-and-after bodies, helpless patients, or symbols of decline.

## Article 8 — One controlled impossibility

Each cover may contain one surreal intervention:

- A shadow behaving differently from its object
- Roots passing through porcelain
- Water held in an impossible shape
- Fabric becoming landscape
- A vessel balancing against expectation

Everything else remains physically convincing.

## Article 9 — The cover is timeless

Avoid visual trends that will date quickly:

- Chrome futurism
- Liquid-metal typography
- Neon gradients
- Vaporwave color
- Glossy 3D abstraction
- Generative particle clouds
- AI-interface aesthetics
- Fashionable filters

## Article 10 — Recognition precedes novelty

A reader should recognize a Vitalspan cover before reading the masthead.

Weekly novelty occurs inside the system, never by abandoning it.

# II. Brand Philosophy

## Editorial character

Vitalspan is:

- Intelligent without being academic
- Beautiful without being decorative
- Scientific without being clinical
- Optimistic without promising outcomes
- Serious without becoming austere
- Human without relying on lifestyle imagery
- Contemporary without chasing trends

## The emotional register

Every cover should produce three sequential reactions:

1. **Recognition:** “I understand the objects.”
2. **Disruption:** “Something about their relationship is unexpected.”
3. **Reflection:** “I want to know what this means.”

The intended mood is **quietly revelatory**.

Not cheerful. Not ominous. Not mystical. Not futuristic.

## The governing idea

Vitalspan portrays longevity as a field of relationships rather than a collection of hacks.

The visual world therefore favors systems, consequences, trade-offs, context, and incomplete transitions.

# III. Visual Language: The Living Still

## Permanent illustration signature

Every image should resemble a hand-painted editorial illustration made with:

- Opaque gouache foundations
- Thin, translucent oil-like glazing
- Cold-press paper texture
- Selective dry-brush edges
- Restrained naturalistic modeling
- Slightly simplified forms
- Quiet surrealism
- Visible but disciplined human irregularity

The result should sit between painting and observed reality.

It must not look like:

- Photography
- Photorealistic rendering
- Flat vector art
- Digital concept art
- Airbrushed advertising
- Children's illustration
- Fantasy art
- 3D rendering

## Shape language

Use:

- Vessels
- Sheets
- Threads
- Seeds
- Reeds
- Stones
- Folds
- Branches
- Ripples
- Layers
- Openings
- Boundaries
- Natural fragments
- Simple domestic objects

Forms should be archetypal rather than brand-specific.

## Perspective

Two viewpoints are permitted:

1. A slightly elevated still-life perspective, approximately 10–20 degrees above the subject.
2. A quiet eye-level view for landscapes, thresholds, and monumental singular objects.

Extreme angles, wide-angle distortion, and cinematic overhead views are prohibited.

## Edge hierarchy

A typical image should contain:

- 15% crisp edges at the intellectual focal point
- 55% softly modeled edges
- 30% lost or atmosphere-absorbed edges

Uniform sharpness makes the work feel digital.

# IV. Lighting

## The permanent light

Vitalspan exists in **cool morning daylight entering from the upper left**.

The light should feel like a north-facing studio window:

- Directional but soft
- Approximately 4,300–4,800K in character
- Long enough to reveal form
- Gentle enough to preserve contemplation
- Never theatrical

## Shadow behavior

Shadows are:

- Soft charcoal, mineral violet, or muted blue-gray
- Transparent rather than black
- Slightly elongated
- Compositionally meaningful

A shadow may occasionally carry the cover’s single surreal intervention.

## Prohibited lighting

Never use:

- Neon rim lighting
- Medical-white illumination
- Sunset sentimentality
- Golden wellness glow
- High-gloss studio lighting
- Dramatic spotlighting
- Black-background chiaroscuro
- Cyberpunk color separation
- Ethereal rays suggesting transcendence

| Verdigris | Rare systems accent | `#71847A` |

## Cover palette rule

Each cover uses:

- One paper ground
- One deep structural neutral
- One biological or mineral color
- No more than one accent

The accent should occupy roughly 5–12% of the image.

## Color behavior

Colors appear mixed from pigments, never selected from a digital color picker. Whites are warm. Blacks are chromatic. Saturation is concentrated only at the focal point.

## Rotation rules

- Do not repeat the same dominant accent on consecutive issues.
- No more than two warm-dominant covers in a row.
- Every four-issue sequence should include at least one cool, one warm, and one nearly monochromatic cover.
- Color must never be the only carrier of meaning.

# VI. Editorial Composition

## Cover geometry

Create the master illustration vertically, with cropping resilience for 4:5, 3:4, square, and horizontal promotional formats.

Maintain:

- Top 18% relatively quiet for the masthead
- Central 70% as the crop-safe narrative area
- Lower 12–15% free of indispensable fine detail
- One immediately legible focal relationship
- Generous negative space

## Visual hierarchy

1. Hero form or relationship
2. Surreal or conceptual event
3. Supporting evidence object
4. Texture and atmosphere

No fifth level is necessary.

## Object count

Preferred:

- One hero object
- One or two supporting forms
- One environmental surface

Five small objects are permitted only for a deliberately low-confidence “five signals” issue.

## Balance

Compositions should be asymmetrical but settled. The focal point generally sits slightly below and to the right of center, allowing the upper-left light and masthead space to remain active.

## Scale

Ordinary objects may be rendered slightly monumental. Tiny scientific imagery should not dominate the cover.

---

# VII. Texture

## Required material character

Every cover should reveal:

- Fine cold-press paper grain
- Thin pigment variation
- Occasional dry-brush transitions
- Subtle underpainting
- Small imperfections in symmetry
- Natural, non-repeating surface variation

## Texture strength

Texture should be visible when viewed closely but disappear into the image at thumbnail size.

## Forbidden texture

Avoid:

- Heavy canvas overlays
- Artificial film grain
- Scratched vintage effects
- Paper-tear collage conventions
- Plastic smoothness
- Waxed 3D surfaces
- Excessive impasto
- Decorative noise added merely to appear handmade

# VIII. Illustration Direction

## Approved modes

Future covers should rotate among five compositional families.

### 1. The Singular Transformation

One familiar object is undergoing one unexpected change.

Best for:

- New findings
- Adaptation
- Reversal
- Emerging interventions

### 2. The Tension Still Life

Two or three objects hold a delicate relationship.

Best for:

- Trade-offs
- Treatment decisions
- Conflicting evidence
- Risk-benefit questions

### 3. The Living System

A small landscape or ecosystem expresses interdependence.

Best for:

- Microbiome
- Metabolism
- Environment
- Multisystem biology

### 4. The Threshold

A boundary, opening, transition, or incomplete passage organizes the image.

Best for:

- Life stages
- Prevention
- Translation into practice
- Evidence maturity

### 5. The Constellation

Several distinct objects coexist without an invented biological connection.

Best for:

- Low-confidence themes
- Weekly signal collections
- Broad research roundups

No compositional family should appear more than twice consecutively.

# IX. Allowed Visual Metaphors

Approved metaphor families include:

- A vessel containing competing states
- A thread becoming stronger, thinner, or more entangled
- Roots responding differently to distinct soils
- A path becoming clearer without reaching a final destination
- Layers revealing an incomplete pattern
- A threshold that remains partly open
- Water finding more than one route
- A flexible structure bending without breaking
- Several measurements failing to align perfectly
- A shadow diverging from expectation
- A surface showing accumulation over time
- A seed or form responding to its environment
- A system balanced by unequal weights
- A bridge or sequence ending before certainty
- Separate objects sharing a physical constraint
- A protective structure imposing a visible cost
- A repaired or adapted surface that retains its history
- A pattern changing with scale or perspective

Metaphors must describe the editorial tension—not invent a biological mechanism.

# XI. Issue Cover System

## Permanent components

Every issue contains:

- The Vitalspan masthead
- One Living Still illustration
- One concise issue title
- Optional restrained issue identifier or date outside the artwork

The illustration itself contains no lettering.

## Cover-study rule

The deterministic cover paper may provide the entry object, but the image represents the issue thesis.

The cover must never visually declare the cover paper to be:

- The most important finding
- A cure
- A definitive answer
- A universal recommendation

## Confidence translation

### High confidence

Use a clear relationship with a controlled but incomplete transformation.

### Medium confidence

Use tension, partial alignment, visible trade-offs, or an unfinished transition.

### Low confidence

Use the Constellation system: five distinct forms, no connecting roots, threads, pathways, or implied shared mechanism.

## Series rhythm

Across eight consecutive covers:

- At least three compositional families must appear.
- No hero object should repeat.
- No dominant accent should repeat consecutively.
- At least two covers should be nearly monochromatic.
- Only one cover may use a visible human trace.
- No more than one cover should use a landscape.

# XII. Weekly Cover Framework

## Step 1 — Establish editorial truth

Before visual ideation, provide:

- Editorial thesis
- Theme confidence
- Theme type
- Supporting source phrases
- Cover-paper role
- Principal uncertainty
- Claims the image must not imply

## Step 2 — Translate the thesis into a relationship

Reduce the theme to one physical tension:

- Protection versus cost
- Signal versus certainty
- Adaptation versus constraint
- Standardization versus population context
- Intervention versus unintended consequence
- Measurement versus lived complexity

## Step 3 — Select one metaphor family

Choose the simplest material relationship capable of carrying the tension.

Do not begin with a biological noun. Begin with the editorial relationship.

## Step 4 — Define the unresolved state

Specify what remains incomplete:

- The vessel is not fully balanced.
- The bridge does not reach the far side.
- The pattern aligns only partially.
- The protective layer leaves another area exposed.
- The path grows clearer but does not arrive.

## Step 5 — Apply the permanent style block

The permanent style block is never rewritten for a particular issue. Only the concept block changes.

## Step 6 — Audit the claim

Ask:

- Does the image imply causation?
- Does it imply a shared mechanism?
- Does it imply clinical benefit?
- Does it visually resolve uncertainty the evidence leaves open?
- Would a scientist recognize the distinction between signal and conclusion?

If yes, revise.

# XIII. Canonical Prompt Framework

## A. Permanent style block

> Create a vertical editorial cover illustration for Vitalspan in its permanent “Living Still” visual language: a restrained hand-painted gouache-and-glaze image on subtly visible cold-press paper. Use simplified naturalistic forms, quiet surrealism, cool morning window light from the upper left, soft chromatic shadows, controlled pigment variation, generous negative space, and one precise conceptual focal point. The composition should feel intelligent, tactile, timeless, and contemplative. Use a bone-paper ground, editorial charcoal, one muted biological or mineral color, and no more than one restrained accent. Preserve quiet space in the upper portion for the masthead. The artwork must feel painted by the same consistent editorial illustrator as every other Vitalspan issue.

## B. Weekly concept block

> This issue concerns: **[EDITORIAL THESIS]**.  
> Theme confidence: **[HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW]**.  
> The central editorial tension is: **[PHYSICAL OR DECISION TENSION]**.  
> Depict: **[HERO OBJECT AND RELATIONSHIP]**.  
> The single controlled impossibility is: **[SURREAL EVENT]**.  
> The image should suggest: **[SUPPORTED INTERPRETATION]**.  
> It must not imply: **[UNSUPPORTED MECHANISM, CAUSATION, OR CLINICAL CONCLUSION]**.  
> Leave the relationship visibly **[INCOMPLETE / CONDITIONAL / UNRESOLVED]**.

## C. Permanent exclusion block

> No typography, lettering, logos, numbers, charts, labels, UI, photography, photorealism, 3D rendering, glossy surfaces, neon, medical-blue gradients, futuristic interfaces, DNA helices, floating organs, pills, doctors, hospital imagery, clocks, hourglasses, fitness imagery, stock wellness symbolism, miraculous transformation, or visual claims of cure or rejuvenation.

## D. Low-confidence template

> Create five visually distinct painted forms arranged as a calm editorial constellation. Unify them only through lighting, palette, scale, and composition. Do not connect them with roots, threads, pathways, shared vessels, or other imagery suggesting a common biological mechanism. The image should communicate several worthwhile scientific signals without pretending they form one conclusion.

# XV. Consistency Rules

## The 80/20 rule

Approximately 80% of every cover is fixed:

- Medium
- Light direction
- Paper character
- Palette behavior
- Emotional register
- Negative space
- Edge hierarchy
- Conceptual restraint
- Absence of typography
- One controlled impossibility

The remaining 20% may change:

- Theme
- Hero object
- Accent color
- Approved composition family
- Specific metaphor
- Degree of visual tension

## Cover rejection criteria

Reject a proposed cover if:

1. It could belong to a health app.
2. It requires the headline to become intelligible.
3. It contains more than one surreal idea.
4. It resembles stock wellness imagery.
5. It implies stronger evidence than the issue supports.
6. It uses a forbidden biomedical cliché.
7. It lacks visible paper or pigment character.
8. It is visually busy at thumbnail size.
9. Its lighting differs from the permanent studio light.
10. Its palette could not sit beside the previous issues.
11. The metaphor cannot be traced to the editorial thesis.
12. The same composition could illustrate almost any health topic.

## Final cover audit

Every approved image must pass:

- **Silhouette test:** recognizable at 80 pixels wide
- **Grayscale test:** hierarchy survives without color
- **Claim test:** no unsupported mechanism or outcome
- **Crop test:** works vertically, square, and horizontally
- **Series test:** unmistakably Vitalspan beside prior issues
- **Novelty test:** contains one fresh idea
- **Restraint test:** nothing can be removed without weakening the image

# XVI. Future-Proof Style Guide

Vitalspan’s identity must be encoded in artistic decisions, not model-specific commands.

Therefore:

- The permanent style block is canonical.
- Model names, seeds, render settings, and aspect-ratio syntax live outside the Art Bible.
- Each approved cover should archive its thesis, prompt, palette, composition family, and exclusions.
- New generation systems must be calibrated against an approved reference set, not allowed to reinterpret the brand freely.
- Trend-driven aesthetics are never added without revising the Art Bible deliberately.
- Future scientific fields should enter through new metaphors, not new visual styles.
- Accessibility must be maintained through tonal contrast, not color alone.
- The last eight covers must always be reviewed together before approving the next.

The ultimate test is simple:

> A Vitalspan cover should look like a beautiful object first, reveal an editorial argument second, and preserve scientific uncertainty throughout.
