# VITALSPAN ART BIBLE

Status: Canonical

Version: 2.0

Owner: Vitalspan

This document is the permanent visual constitution of Vitalspan. Implementations, prompts, image generation, and cover review must follow it unless this file is intentionally revised.

---

## Version 2.0 change record

Version 2.0 makes a deliberate editorial change:

- A weekly Brief still contains exactly five selected articles.
- One of those five is explicitly evaluated and selected as the cover story.
- The cover expresses the chosen article's most compelling scientific story, not a generic thesis synthesized across all five.
- The other four articles may influence the issue title or editor's letter, but they do not have to appear in the core artwork.
- Four visual families are now explicit: Living Tapestry, Landscape, Architecture, and Living Still.
- Living Tapestry is preferred when supported by the science.
- Architecture is limited to named scientific structural relationships.
- Living Still is exceptional and must never be a confidence-based default.
- Repeated recipes involving vessels, bowls, stones, linen, seeds, paper, and tabletops have been removed from the generative vocabulary.
- The founding cover remains a quality benchmark, not a compositional template.

Preserved from earlier versions: scientific restraint, human dignity, material finish, lighting discipline, typography separation, crop safety, consistency, and review standards.

---

## I. Editorial constitution

### 1. Vitalspan depicts inquiry, not wellness

Vitalspan is an editorial longevity magazine. Covers should suggest observation, adaptation, consequence, time, biological relationship, and incomplete knowledge.

They must never resemble:

- A health-tracking product
- A hospital campaign
- A supplement advertisement
- A fitness brand
- A biotechnology pitch deck
- A generic "future of medicine" image

### 2. Five articles, one cover story, one visual sentence

The weekly issue contains exactly five selected articles. Before concept work begins, the system evaluates all five and chooses the strongest cover story using:

- Scientific significance
- Visual potential
- Reader curiosity
- Editorial relevance to longevity
- Credible evidence
- Freshness or surprise
- Capacity for a memorable visual story without overstating the evidence

The deterministic cover article is a nomination, not an automatic result. It must be explicitly confirmed or overturned with a reason. Production selection is never random.

The artwork visualizes the selected article's single strongest scientific story. A cover must form one visual sentence and remain legible as one macro silhouette at thumbnail size.

### 3. Story before family, relationship before form

The permanent order is:

> Five selected articles → Cover-story selection → Scientific story extraction → Evidence limitation → Four to six concepts → Concept selection → Visual-family selection → Direction → Prompt → One render

No visual family, hero object, prop, palette, or composition is selected before the scientific relationship and visual event are established.

### 4. Scientific specificity is mandatory

The core image must express an article-specific scientific story. Qualifications such as "uncertainty remains" or "promising evidence meets its limits" may constrain the image, but they may not become the primary story unless the source article is genuinely about evidence methodology.

Reject a concept that could illustrate an unrelated longevity article without meaningful change.

### 5. Beauty must not overstate evidence

Visual resolution must match evidentiary resolution.

- Strong evidence may support a clear relationship or stable event.
- Limited evidence should retain visible incompleteness, unevenness, separation, or unresolved attribution.
- Unsupported mechanisms, causes, cures, clinical recommendations, durability, and generalizability must never be invented.

Confidence controls claim strength and the degree of visual closure. It does not force minimalism or a still life.

### 6. One memorable event

Every cover needs one relationship, transformation, or event that remains memorable after the reader looks away. Detail may reward a second look, but it cannot rescue a weak macro idea.

The intended sequence is:

1. Recognition
2. Wonder
3. Interpretation
4. Reflection

Curiosity arrives before calm. A cover that is merely tasteful fails.

## II. Four visual families

Family selection occurs only after concept selection. Use this preference when the science supports it:

1. Living Tapestry
2. Landscape
3. Architecture
4. Living Still

This preference is editorial guidance, not a quota.

### 1. Living Tapestry — preferred when scientifically justified

A rich, unified biological-poetic world in one continuous visual language.

Use for:

- Connected biological systems
- Microbiome and metabolism
- Neurobiology and cardiovascular biology
- Muscle physiology
- Cellular adaptation
- Multisystem or cross-scale relationships
- Mechanisms supported by the selected source

Required qualities:

- Integrated organic systems
- Macro-to-micro transitions
- Branching, folding, vascular, neural, cellular, botanical, or ecological rhythms derived from the science
- Scientific linework embedded in material texture
- Symbolic, transformed biology rather than textbook anatomy
- Rich but disciplined detail
- One coherent visual ecosystem
- One memorable macro silhouette
- Close-view scientific discovery

Living Tapestry may contain one system or several. It does not require a gut-heart-brain arrangement or multiple organs.

Reject it when abundance becomes spectacle, when continuity implies an unsupported mechanism, or when biological motifs have no source-grounded role.

### 2. Landscape

Landscape turns the scientific finding into a natural environment or process.

Use for:

- Adaptation
- Accumulation
- Resilience and recovery
- Environmental determinants
- Progression over time
- Ecological relationships
- Competing or reconnecting pathways

The environmental event must map directly to the finding. Terrain, flow, thaw, roots, erosion, recovery, pressure, or succession cannot be decorative scenery.

Reject a landscape if removing the source article would leave an equally plausible travel image.

### 3. Architecture — rare and controlled

Architecture is a scientific relationship embodied in space. It is not a visual style.

Use only for a named structural idea such as:

- Barrier or permeability
- Access
- Compartmentalization
- Threshold
- Scaffolding
- Structural failure
- Protected versus exposed regions
- Translation from one stage to another

Every wall, opening, division, support, or boundary must perform that specific scientific relationship.

Reject:

- Generic planes or blocks
- Corridors without scientific meaning
- Monumental voids
- Fashionable geometry
- Architecture-magazine imagery
- Any space in which the science disappears

### 4. Living Still — exceptional

Living Still is a restrained physical metaphor using ordinary materials. It is used only when one physical relationship communicates the article unusually clearly.

Before selection, state one sentence proving why the physical relationship is uniquely appropriate to the scientific story.

Living Still is never selected merely because confidence is medium or because a minimal image feels premium.

Reject:

- Tasteful prop arrangements
- Generic bowls, vessels, stones, linen, seeds, paper, or tabletops
- Anomalous shadows used as a reusable trick
- A scene that needs written explanation to become meaningful
- A collection of objects standing in for the other four articles

An ordinary material may appear only when the selected source story specifically justifies its scientific role.

## III. Concept standard

Generate four to six genuinely different concepts for the selected cover story. Return the strongest two and select the strongest concept before family selection.

Every concept states:

- The visual story
- The scientific relationship
- The visual event
- The scientific anchor
- Why it will be memorable
- Suitable visual families
- Implications the image must not create

Reject concepts that are:

- Generic evidence metaphors
- Interchangeable across unrelated science stories
- Decorative
- Object inventories
- Architecture without scientific meaning
- Vague AI abstraction
- Direct copies of the founding artwork
- Impossible to understand without reading the prompt

A hero object is optional, never required.

## IV. Permanent illustration signature

Vitalspan covers use a hand-painted editorial language:

- Opaque gouache foundations
- Thin translucent glazing
- Cold-press paper tooth
- Selective dry-brush edges
- Restrained naturalistic modeling
- Simplified, transformed forms
- Scientific linework embedded in material texture
- Controlled human irregularity

The result sits between painting and observed reality.

It must not look like:

- Photography or photorealistic rendering
- Flat vector art
- Glossy digital concept art
- Plastic 3D
- Airbrushed fantasy
- A medical diagram
- A generic AI particle field

### Structural continuity

Continuity may be carried by branching, folding, rhythm, color migration, edge behavior, material transformation, repeated scale, environmental flow, or spatial logic. It must arise from the selected scientific relationship.

### Macro and micro hierarchy

Every cover supports two readings:

- At thumbnail scale: one clear, memorable silhouette or field structure.
- At close range: selective scientific discovery through fibers, membranes, vascular or neural linework, cellular traces, ecological textures, or another source-grounded detail language.

Fine detail is unequally distributed. Complexity concentrates where meaning concentrates.

### Depth

Depth comes from overlapping opaque and translucent layers, edge hierarchy, atmospheric recess, lost and found contours, and changes of scale. It need not imply a literal room or horizon.

## V. Color and light

### Color

Use restrained, natural, mineral, botanical, bodily, or environmental color. Saturation is selective. One controlled accent may concentrate attention, but color must establish hierarchy rather than decorate every form.

Avoid:

- Neon gradients
- Uniform glow
- Chrome futurism
- Vaporwave color
- Indiscriminate saturation
- Equal chromatic emphasis everywhere

### Light

The canonical light is cool morning daylight entering from the upper-left, approximately 4300–4800K, with soft chromatic shadows.

Light reveals material, depth, and uncertainty. It must not appear as a digital aura or imply transcendence.

## VI. Evidence and safety rules

### Claim discipline

Before concept generation, record:

- Central finding
- Why it matters
- Editorial question
- Principal uncertainty
- Prohibited claims
- One vivid visual-story sentence

The final direction and prompt repeat the relevant uncertainty and prohibited implications.

Never imply without source support:

- Causation
- Cure, reversal, or prevention
- Clinical recommendation
- A shared mechanism
- Equal contribution from components
- Clinical importance
- Durability
- Generalization beyond the studied population or setting

### Human dignity

Aging is shown as adaptation, depth, time, patina, knowledge, or transformation—never deterioration for shock value. People are not diagnoses, helpless patients, before-and-after bodies, or symbols of decline.

### No language in generated art

Generated artwork contains no:

- Masthead or headline
- Labels or captions
- Numbers or scientific notation
- Charts
- Logos, signatures, or watermarks
- Pseudo-lettering

Typography belongs to the layout layer.

### Timelessness

Avoid fashionable filters, AI-interface aesthetics, liquid-metal effects, synthetic futurism, and visual trends likely to date quickly.

## VII. Composition and crop safety

Master format: portrait 3:4 at 1152 × 1536.

- Upper 18% remains quiet for editorial layout.
- Essential meaning stays inside the central 70%.
- Lower 12–15% contains no essential detail.
- The macro argument survives at approximately 80 pixels wide.
- Negative space participates in the composition; it is not leftover emptiness.
- Cropping must not remove the scientific event or evidence qualification.

An integrated visual field does not require a central object. A single silhouette may emerge from a system, landscape, spatial relation, or material event.

## VIII. Founding-cover relationship

`assets/brief-cover-editorial.jpg` is the founding quality reference.

Inherit:

- Richness and continuity
- Macro/micro depth
- Scientific texture
- Second-look curiosity
- Material specificity
- Selective emphasis
- A memorable silhouette

Do not copy:

- The gut-heart-brain arrangement
- Its anatomical or botanical inventory
- Its serpentine composition
- Its dark ground
- Its exact palette
- Its location of focal masses

The benchmark is advisory. A future cover can meet it with one biological system, a landscape, a scientifically meaningful space, or an exceptional physical relationship.

---

## IX. Review gate

A cover fails if any answer is no:

1. Was one cover story explicitly selected from exactly five articles?
2. Does the artwork express that article's specific scientific story?
3. Is the evidence limitation visible without replacing the story?
4. Were four to six distinct concepts considered before family selection?
5. Is the chosen family justified by the scientific relationship?
6. Does the image have one memorable macro silhouette?
7. Does close viewing reveal source-grounded scientific detail?
8. Are unsupported claims prohibited?
9. Are the other four articles absent from the core composition unless independently required by the cover story?
10. Is the artwork recognizably Vitalspan without copying the founding cover?
11. Is all generated typography absent?
12. Is the result more than tasteful?

Human approval remains required before publication.
