# SCREEN BIBLE

## Purpose

This document translates the Product Bible into actual product experiences.

The Product Bible defines what Vitalspan believes.

The Screen Bible defines how those beliefs appear on every screen.

Every screen should answer a clear user question,

support the Product Philosophy,

and contribute to the long-term product vision.

If a screen cannot clearly explain why it exists,

it should not exist.

---

# Design Philosophy

Every screen should help users:

Understand more.

Decide more confidently.

Take one meaningful action.

Leave feeling more capable than when they arrived.

Screens are not collections of features.

They are experiences designed to answer human questions.

# 3. TODAY

## Purpose

The Today screen is the starting point of the Vitalspan experience.

It should answer one simple question:

"What should I focus on today?"

Users should never feel overwhelmed by data.

Instead,

Today should summarize,

prioritize,

and guide.

It transforms years of health information into today's most meaningful actions.

---

## User Question

"What matters most for my health today?"

---

## Success

Before leaving the Today screen,

users should:

• Understand today's priorities.

• Feel confident about what to do next.

• See meaningful progress.

• Feel motivated,

not overwhelmed.

---

## Core Philosophy

Today is not a dashboard.

It is a daily health briefing.

The purpose is not to display everything.

The purpose is to highlight what matters today.


## Information Hierarchy

Information should compete for attention as little as possible.

The Today screen should present information in order of importance,

not in order of feature availability.

Users should understand today's most important priorities within a few seconds.

The hierarchy should always be:

1. Today's Health Brief

2. Top Priorities

3. Key Insight

4. Today's Progress

5. Quick Actions

Everything else should remain secondary.

If information does not help users decide what matters today,

it should not appear on the Today screen.

## Screen Structure

The Today screen should feel like a morning briefing,

not a health report.

Each section should naturally answer the next question.

Good morning.

↓

What should I know today?

↓

What deserves my attention?

↓

What should I do?

↓

How am I progressing?

↓

What else would I like to explore?

The screen should feel complete after one scroll.

Users should not need to search for today's priorities.

## First Impression

Within five seconds,

users should understand:

• How they are doing today.

• What deserves attention.

• What action is most valuable.

The experience should create clarity,

not curiosity.

Users should never wonder where to begin.

## Primary Components

The Today screen should contain only the information necessary to guide today's decisions.

Every component must answer a clear question.

### 1. Greeting

Welcome the user naturally.

The greeting should feel personal,

not generic.

---

### 2. Today's Health Brief

A short AI-generated summary of today's health.

This is the most important section of the screen.

It should combine the user's recent health data,

ongoing trends,

and current priorities into a concise daily briefing.

---

### 3. Top Priorities

Show the two or three actions that will have the greatest impact today.

Priorities should always explain why they matter.

---

### 4. Key Insight

Highlight one meaningful observation.

Examples include:

• An improving trend.

• A declining habit.

• A new achievement.

• An unusual change.

Only one insight should be shown at a time.

---

### 5. Today's Progress

Provide a simple overview of today's completed health activities.

Progress should encourage consistency,

not perfection.

---

### 6. Quick Actions

Offer the fastest path to common actions,

such as logging water,

starting a workout,

recording supplements,

or viewing today's plan.

Quick Actions should reduce friction,

not increase options.


## AI Opportunities

Artificial Intelligence should not dominate the Today screen.

It should quietly improve every part of the experience.

The primary role of AI is to reduce thinking,

not increase reading.

AI should help users:

• Summarize today's health.

• Explain why priorities matter.

• Identify meaningful patterns.

• Connect information across different health domains.

• Recommend the next best action.

AI should always be concise.

Users should never feel like they are reading a report.

They should feel like they are receiving guidance.

## Empty State

The first experience should feel hopeful,

not empty.

If little or no health data is available,

the Today screen should focus on helping users begin.

Examples include:

• Connecting Apple Health.

• Completing a health profile.

• Logging today's first activity.

• Taking a short health assessment.

Empty states should encourage progress,

never highlight missing data.
## Premium Experience

Premium should make the Today screen feel more personal,

not more crowded.

Premium enhancements may include:

• More personalized AI Health Briefs.

• Deeper explanations.

• Trend predictions.

• Cross-domain health insights.

• Long-term pattern recognition.

Premium should increase understanding,

not simply unlock more information.
## Future Expansion

The Today screen should remain stable as Vitalspan evolves.

Future features should strengthen the daily briefing experience,

rather than compete for attention.

New capabilities should naturally integrate into existing sections,

avoiding additional complexity.

The Today screen should become increasingly intelligent,

not increasingly crowded.

## User Journey

The Today experience should require almost no thinking.

A user opening Vitalspan should naturally move through the following journey.

Greeting

↓

Understand today's health

↓

Identify today's priorities

↓

Learn one meaningful insight

↓

Review today's progress

↓

Take one meaningful action

↓

Continue with the day

The experience should feel calm,

focused,

and complete.

Users should leave within one or two minutes,

feeling more confident than when they arrived.

## User Emotions

The Today screen should intentionally create the following emotional progression.

Arrival

"I wonder how I'm doing today."

↓

Relief

"Okay, I understand."

↓

Confidence

"I know what matters."

↓

Motivation

"I can do this."

↓

Completion

"I'm ready to continue my day."

The screen should never create stress,

confusion,

or decision fatigue.

## Content Rules

Every piece of content shown on the Today screen should satisfy at least one of the following goals.

• Increase understanding.

• Encourage meaningful action.

• Reinforce positive progress.

• Reduce uncertainty.

If a piece of information serves none of these goals,

it should not appear.

Information is valuable only when it helps users make better decisions today.

## Decision Rules

Whenever multiple health events compete for attention,

the Today screen should prioritize them using the following order.

1. Immediate health concerns.

2. Today's highest-impact opportunity.

3. Long-term health trends.

4. Positive progress worth celebrating.

5. Routine reminders.

The most meaningful information should always appear first,

regardless of feature category.

## Personalization

The Today screen should become more personal over time.

New users should receive simple,

high-confidence guidance.

Long-term users should receive increasingly personalized insights based on their own health history.

The experience should feel like it remembers the user,

not like it simply displays data.

Every day should feel slightly different,

because every day the user is different.

## Cross-Screen Navigation

The Today screen should never become a destination for deep analysis.

Instead,

it should act as the starting point for deeper exploration.

Every section should naturally guide users to the appropriate screen when they want more detail.

Today answers:

"What matters?"

Other screens answer:

"Why?"

"How?"

"What changed?"

## Success Indicators

A successful Today experience can be recognized when users:

• Understand today's priorities within seconds.

• Rarely feel overwhelmed.

• Frequently complete recommended actions.

• Return because the screen consistently provides value.

Success is measured by confidence,

not by time spent on the screen.

## Anti Goals

The Today screen should never:

• Display every available metric.

• Encourage endless scrolling.

• Require users to interpret complex health data.

• Compete with other screens.

• Create anxiety through excessive alerts.

• Feel like a dashboard full of widgets.

If the screen begins to do any of these,

it is moving away from its intended purpose.

# 4. HEALTH

## Purpose

The Health screen is the permanent memory of the user's health.

It helps users understand their body,

recognize long-term patterns,

and make sense of years of health information.

Unlike the Today screen,

Health is not focused on today's priorities.

It is focused on long-term understanding.

---

## User Question

"What do I know about my body?"

---

## Success

Before leaving the Health screen,

users should:

• Better understand their current health.

• Recognize meaningful long-term trends.

• Feel confident interpreting their health data.

• Know where further exploration is needed.

Health should create understanding,

not information overload.

---

## Core Philosophy

Health is not a collection of disconnected metrics.

It is one connected system.

Every health domain should contribute to a broader understanding of the user's overall wellbeing.

The Health screen should encourage users to see connections,

not isolated numbers.

## Information Hierarchy

The Health screen should prioritize understanding over measurement.

Information should be presented in the following order.

1. Overall Health Overview

2. Significant Long-Term Trends

3. Key Health Domains

4. Historical Data

5. Detailed Metrics

Users should understand the big picture before exploring individual measurements.


## First Impression

Within a few seconds,

users should understand:

• Their overall health status.

• Whether they are improving.

• Which health areas deserve attention.

The Health screen should answer,

"How am I doing overall?"

before answering,

"How many steps did I take?"

## User Journey

The Health experience should help users gradually understand their health,

from the big picture to the smallest detail.

Overall Health

↓

Long-Term Trends

↓

Health Domains

↓

Detailed Metrics

↓

Personal Insights

↓

Meaningful Understanding

Users should never feel lost inside their health data.

Every interaction should increase clarity.
## User Emotions

The Health screen should intentionally create the following emotional progression.

Curiosity

"I want to understand my health."

↓

Clarity

"Now I see the bigger picture."

↓

Confidence

"I understand what these numbers mean."

↓

Ownership

"I know where I can improve."

Users should leave feeling informed,

not overwhelmed.
## Primary Components

The Health screen should organize health information into meaningful experiences,

rather than isolated measurements.

### 1. Overall Health Summary

Provide a concise overview of the user's current health status.

The summary should connect multiple health domains into one understandable narrative.

---

### 2. Long-Term Trends

Highlight meaningful changes over weeks,

months,

or years.

The emphasis should be on direction,

not daily fluctuations.

---

### 3. Health Domains

Organize health into clear categories such as:

• Recovery

• Sleep

• Activity

• Nutrition

• Heart Health

• Biomarkers

Each domain should contribute to the user's overall understanding.

---

### 4. Personal Insights

Present one or more AI-generated insights that connect information across different domains.

Insights should explain relationships,

not simply describe numbers.

---

### 5. Deep Exploration

Allow users to explore detailed history,

charts,

and reports when desired.

Details should always support understanding,

never replace it.
## Decision Rules

The Health screen should prioritize information using the following order.

1. Major long-term improvements.

2. Areas needing attention.

3. Stable health domains.

4. Historical exploration.

5. Raw measurements.

Users should always understand the story before seeing the data.

## AI Opportunities

Artificial Intelligence should help users understand relationships,

not simply explain individual metrics.

AI should naturally answer questions such as:

• Why has this changed?

• Which health areas influence each other?

• What deserves attention first?

• Which improvements are having the greatest impact?

• What is the next best opportunity?

AI should transform disconnected health data into one understandable story.

## Empty State

The Health screen should never feel empty.

If little health information is available,

the experience should focus on helping users build their personal health profile.

Suggested actions include:

• Connect Apple Health.

• Import laboratory results.

• Complete baseline assessments.

• Log daily health activities.

Users should feel that every new piece of information strengthens their long-term health story.

## Premium Experience

Premium should deepen understanding,

not simply reveal additional numbers.

Examples include:

• Cross-domain AI analysis.

• Long-term trend interpretation.

• Personalized health forecasts.

• Advanced biomarker explanations.

• Preventive health insights.

Premium should provide greater clarity,

not greater complexity.

## Future Expansion

The Health screen should naturally grow alongside the user's health journey.

Future capabilities may include:

• Continuous health risk monitoring.

• Advanced biological aging models.

• Family health history.

• Clinical report integration.

• Preventive health simulations.

New functionality should strengthen the overall health narrative,

never fragment it.

## Anti Goals

The Health screen should never:

• Become a list of disconnected metrics.

• Prioritize numbers over understanding.

• Encourage unnecessary comparison.

• Require medical knowledge to interpret.

• Hide important insights behind complexity.

• Feel like a clinical database.

The Health experience should always simplify,

connect,

and explain.

# 5. PLAN

## Purpose

The Plan screen transforms health understanding into meaningful action.

It helps users improve their health through clear,

personalized,

and achievable guidance.

Unlike the Health screen,

Plan is not about understanding.

It is about progress.

---

## User Question

"What should I do next?"

---

## Success

Before leaving the Plan screen,

users should:

• Know exactly what to do next.

• Feel confident that the plan is achievable.

• Understand why each recommendation matters.

• Feel motivated to take action today.

The Plan screen should remove uncertainty,

not create pressure.

---

## Core Philosophy

Knowledge without action creates little value.

The Plan screen exists to bridge the gap between understanding and improvement.

Every recommendation should feel:

Personal.

Practical.

Achievable.

Meaningful.

Users should never feel that they are following a generic checklist.

They should feel that the plan was created specifically for them.


## Information Hierarchy

Recommendations should be prioritized by impact,

not quantity.

The hierarchy should always be:

1. Today's Highest-Impact Action

2. Current Health Goals

3. Personalized Recommendations

4. Habits In Progress

5. Completed Achievements

Users should immediately understand where their effort will create the greatest benefit.

## First Impression

Within a few seconds,

users should understand:

• What is today's most valuable action.

• Why it matters.

• How difficult it is.

• How much progress they are making.

The screen should answer:

"What is the best thing I can do today?"

## User Journey

The Plan experience should naturally guide users through action.

Today's Priority

↓

Understand Why

↓

Choose An Action

↓

Complete The Action

↓

Track Progress

↓

Build Consistency

The journey should always feel achievable.

Small wins should happen every day.

## User Emotions

The Plan screen should intentionally create the following emotional progression.

Clarity

"I know what I should do."

↓

Confidence

"This feels achievable."

↓

Motivation

"I want to start."

↓

Momentum

"I'm making progress."

↓

Pride

"I'm becoming healthier."

The Plan experience should always create hope,

never guilt.
## Primary Components

The Plan screen should organize recommendations into actionable experiences.

### 1. Today's Biggest Opportunity

Present the single action that is expected to create the greatest positive impact today.

This should always be the primary focus.

---

### 2. Why It Matters

Explain why today's recommendation is important.

Users should understand the reasoning before taking action.

---

### 3. Action Plan

Break the recommendation into simple,

achievable steps.

Large goals should become small actions.

---

### 4. Progress Tracking

Show meaningful progress toward current health goals.

Progress should emphasize consistency,

not perfection.

---

### 5. Habit Building

Support long-term behavior change through sustainable routines.

Habits should feel achievable,

not demanding.

## AI Opportunities

Artificial Intelligence should function as a personal health coach.

AI should help users:

• Prioritize recommendations.

• Explain why each action matters.

• Adapt plans based on progress.

• Adjust recommendations when circumstances change.

• Celebrate meaningful improvements.

AI should guide,

never command.
## Content Rules

Recommendations should always be:

Personal.

Specific.

Actionable.

Evidence-informed.

Realistic.

Users should never receive vague advice such as:

"Be healthier."

Every recommendation should clearly explain:

What to do.

Why to do it.

Expected impact.

Estimated effort.
## Decision Rules

When multiple recommendations are available,

the Plan screen should prioritize them using the following order.

1. Highest health impact.

2. Highest personal relevance.

3. Lowest implementation difficulty.

4. Long-term consistency.

5. Additional optimization opportunities.

Users should always feel that today's recommendations are achievable.
## Empty State

If no personalized recommendations are available,

the Plan screen should guide users toward creating one.

Examples include:

• Complete your health profile.

• Connect Apple Health.

• Import laboratory results.

• Set your first health goal.

The experience should invite users to begin,

not punish them for missing data.
## Premium Experience

Premium should provide more intelligent planning,

not more tasks.

Examples include:

• Adaptive health plans.

• Dynamic goal adjustment.

• Long-term coaching.

• AI-generated weekly plans.

• Personalized optimization strategies.

Premium should reduce decision making,

not increase it.
## Future Expansion

The Plan experience should evolve into a lifelong coaching system.

Future capabilities may include:

• Seasonal health plans.

• Travel-aware recommendations.

• Family health planning.

• Preventive health roadmaps.

• Clinical follow-up plans.

Growth should increase personalization,

not complexity.
## Anti Goals

The Plan screen should never:

• Become a checklist of unrelated tasks.

• Overwhelm users with recommendations.

• Create guilt for incomplete goals.

• Recommend unrealistic lifestyle changes.

• Ignore personal preferences.

• Feel like homework.

The Plan experience should always feel supportive,

achievable,

and personal.

# 8. SHARED COMPONENTS

## Purpose

Shared Components provide a consistent language across the entire product.

Users should recognize familiar interaction patterns regardless of which screen they are using.

Consistency should reduce cognitive effort and increase confidence.

---

## Design Principles

Every shared component should:

• Solve one clear problem.

• Look and behave consistently.

• Be reusable across multiple screens.

• Prioritize clarity over decoration.

• Support accessibility by default.

Components should never exist simply for visual variety.

---

## Core Components

### Health Card

Used to summarize the current state of a health domain.

Appears throughout Today, Health and Plan.

---

### Insight Card

Highlights an important observation or relationship.

Always explains why the insight matters.

---

### Recommendation Card

Displays one actionable recommendation.

Must always answer:

• What should I do?

• Why?

• Expected impact.

---

### AI Card

Represents AI-generated insights, explanations or coaching.

AI Cards should always communicate confidence, transparency and personalization.

---

### Metric Card

Displays a single measurement with supporting context.

Raw numbers should never appear without explanation.

---

### Progress Card

Visualizes long-term progress toward a health goal.

Progress should emphasize consistency rather than perfection.

---

### Chart

Charts exist to explain trends,

not impress users.

Every chart should answer a question.

---

### Timeline

Shows meaningful health events over time.

Users should understand change,

not simply chronology.

---

### Bottom Sheet

Used for quick actions,

secondary information,

or lightweight workflows.

Should never replace a full screen when deep interaction is required.

---

### Dialog

Reserved for confirmations,

important decisions,

or destructive actions.

Dialogs should never interrupt learning unnecessarily.

---

### Empty State

Every empty state should educate,

encourage,

or guide.

An empty screen should always suggest a meaningful next step.

---

### Loading State

Loading should reassure users that meaningful work is happening.

Skeletons should reflect the final layout whenever possible.

---

### Error State

Errors should explain:

• What happened.

• Why it happened.

• What users can do next.

Never display technical language.

---

### Navigation

Navigation should always reinforce the product journey.

Users should rarely wonder where to go next.

---

## Component Philosophy

Every component should answer one question.

If a component attempts to communicate multiple ideas,

it should probably become multiple components.

Shared components should create familiarity,

trust,

and predictability across the entire product.

---

## AI Navigation

AI should act as connective tissue across the entire application.

AI may recommend:

• Related health metrics.

• Relevant lessons.

• Appropriate plans.

• Connected health domains.

• Follow-up questions.

AI should reduce navigation effort.

---

## Navigation Principles

Every screen should answer:

"What should I do next?"

Users should never reach a dead end.

Every important insight should have a natural next action.

Every recommendation should have an explanation.

Every lesson should connect back to the user's own health.

Every completed action should reinforce progress.

---

## Product Loop

Understand

↓

Plan

↓

Act

↓

Measure

↓

Learn

↓

Improve

↓

Repeat

This loop represents the core operating model of Vitalspan.

All new features should strengthen this loop rather than create parallel experiences.

---

## Anti Goals

Cross-screen navigation should never:

• Force unnecessary steps.

• Create circular dead ends.

• Duplicate information.

• Interrupt the user's flow.

• Require users to search for the next action.

Navigation should always feel effortless,

predictable,

and purposeful.
