# Phase 5.0C — VO₂max Reference Standard

**Document type:** Official normative scientific specification  
**Scientific platform:** Vitalspan  
**Review date:** 18 July 2026  
**Status:** Approved scientific standard  
**Final production recommendation:** **Production Ready — conditional**

## 1. Decision

Vitalspan may provide normative interpretation of eligible VO₂max-domain
measurements under this standard. Production readiness is deliberately narrow:

- The **2022 updated FRIEND Registry standards** are the official first-line
  reference for apparently healthy United States adults aged 20–89 whose directly
  measured CPET endpoint, sex stratum, age stratum, and treadmill or cycle modality
  match the published reference.
- FRIEND is the strongest operational national reference reviewed, but it is not a
  universal human standard. No scientifically adequate all-age, all-region global
  CPET reference currently exists.
- Vitalspan must use a **versioned family of regional and modality-specific
  references**, not one global table. A regional reference may be activated only
  after scientific admission and validation for its intended population.
- **Age plus sex is the minimum demographic stratification.** Test modality,
  measurement endpoint, measurement quality, reference population, and reference
  version are also mandatory parts of the interpretation identity.
- **Percentile is the official normative output.** It describes position within a
  named reference distribution; it is not a diagnosis, probability of death, or
  statement of biological normality.
- Vitalspan must not adopt “Very Poor,” “Poor,” “Fair,” “Average,” “Good,”
  “Excellent,” or “Superior” as official clinical classes. Their boundaries vary
  by source and are generally convention- or percentile-derived rather than
  validated outcome thresholds.
- No universal VO₂max value separates healthy from unhealthy, long-lived from
  short-lived, or low from high mortality risk. Mortality and hospitalization
  associations are strong and graded, but published cut points are cohort- and
  purpose-specific.
- An unmatched person or measurement receives **no normative percentile**. The raw
  measurement remains valid for its authorized purpose; Vitalspan must not silently
  substitute a U.S., European, or estimated reference.

This standard authorizes scientific interpretation policy only. It does not
authorize a calculation, equation, score, biological-age transformation, diagnosis,
clinical recommendation, user interface, or production implementation.

## 2. Primary questions

| Question | Official answer |
| --- | --- |
| Which normative reference population should Vitalspan adopt? | A versioned, modality-matched regional family. Launch with the updated 2022 FRIEND apparently healthy U.S. adult cohort for eligible U.S. adults. Admit other regional datasets separately after validation; do not pool them by default. |
| Which international dataset has the strongest scientific support? | FRIEND has the strongest combination of sample size, direct gas measurement, sex and age coverage, both major modalities, multicentre quality control, clinical visibility, and continuing governance. The 2026 ERS/ATS-endorsed technical standard found that available international data were too heterogeneous to generate generalizable global ranges. |

| Age, sex, region, or another grouping? | Age plus source-recorded sex is the minimum. Region/reference population and test modality are mandatory matching variables. Endpoint, measurement method, and health-population status must also match. Ethnicity must be used for representativeness and fairness evaluation, not as an automatic biological correction. |
| Are mortality thresholds suitable for production interpretation? | Not as general-population normative thresholds. The association is continuous and published bands differ by study. Disease-specific clinical thresholds remain valid only inside their original clinical pathway and full CPET interpretation. |

## 3. Scope and normative language

**Must** and **must not** identify requirements. **Should** identifies the preferred
scientific policy where an exception requires documented review. **May** identifies
an allowed option.

This document governs:

- selection and admission of normative VO₂max and VO₂peak references;
- matching of a measurement to a reference population;
- age, sex, region, modality, endpoint, and population-status policy;
- percentile and classification policy;
- separation of normative interpretation from mortality and clinical thresholds;
- reference provenance, versioning, validation, monitoring, and retirement; and
- the scientific boundaries for production use.

It does not replace the measurement requirements in the Phase 5.0B Measurement
Standard. A reference cannot upgrade a measurement. An estimate remains an estimate,
and VO₂peak remains VO₂peak, regardless of the reference against which it is
displayed.

## 4. Review method

### 4.1 Review approach

The review was updated through **18 July 2026**. It examined society standards,
systematic reviews, direct-CPET registries, population cohorts, conventional fitness
classification sources, and mortality and hospitalization cohorts. Priority was
given to peer-reviewed primary publications and official society sources.

This is a structured scientific review, not a prospectively registered systematic
review. It does not claim exhaustive study-level screening.

### 4.2 Selection criteria

A candidate normative dataset was favored when it had:

- direct respiratory gas measurement rather than workload-derived estimation;
- a clearly defined apparently healthy reference population;
- substantial coverage across adult age and both published sex strata;
- explicit treadmill or cycle modality;
- documented equipment calibration and laboratory quality control;
- transparent inclusion, exclusion, endpoint, and maximal-effort criteria;
- percentiles or centiles rather than only a population mean;
- geographic and clinical transportability evidence;
- independent validation or a separate validation cohort; and
- active scientific governance and current clinical use.

### 4.3 Evidence confidence

| Level | Meaning in this standard |
| --- | --- |
| **High** | Direct CPET, large and relevant population, transparent methodology, strong quality control, and concordant external or standards evidence. |
| **Moderate** | Scientifically useful direct data with material selection, regional, validation, modality, age, or laboratory limitations. |
| **Low** | Estimated fitness, weak representativeness, opaque derivation, outdated source, or poor transportability for the proposed use. |
| **Insufficient** | Evidence does not support production normative interpretation for the proposed population or purpose. |

These are qualitative scientific judgments, not scores.

## 5. Reference concepts

### 5.1 Normative is not clinical

A normative percentile answers: **where does this result fall within a specified
reference distribution?** It does not answer:

- whether the person has disease;
- why the value is high or low;
- the person's probability of hospitalization or death;
- whether exercise is safe;
- whether the value is adequate for an occupation or sport; or
- what treatment or training the person needs.

A healthy-reference distribution also does not define a diagnostic “normal range.”
Reference cohorts can be selected, fitter, less diverse, or methodologically
different from the person being assessed. The 2026 ERS technical standard further
warns that a fixed percentage of a predicted value is not equivalent to a
statistically derived lower limit of normal
([ERS technical standard](https://publications.ersnet.org/content/erj/67/6/2502463)).

### 5.2 Reference identity

Every normative interpretation must identify all of the following:

- reference publication and immutable Vitalspan reference version;
- country or regional population represented;
- apparently healthy or disease-specific cohort status;
- age scope and age handling method;
- source-recorded sex stratum;
- treadmill or cycle modality;
- direct VO₂max or VO₂peak endpoint and effort criterion;
- absolute or body-mass-normalized expression;
- source laboratory and measurement eligibility class; and
- the reference's supported and unsupported populations.

Changing any of these can change the interpretation. A percentile without its
reference identity is scientifically incomplete.

### 5.3 Measurement eligibility

Official normative percentiles are authorized only for provenance-complete,
quality-controlled direct CPET results that match the admitted reference. The
Phase 5.0B Gold Standard direct VO₂max is eligible. A Clinical Grade direct VO₂peak
may be eligible only against a reference that reports a scientifically compatible
peak endpoint and effort rule.

Apple Health, Apple Watch, Garmin, Polar, COROS, Fitbit, WHOOP, field-test,
workload-derived, and non-exercise estimates must not be placed into a direct-CPET
normative distribution. A manufacturer-provided “fitness level,” percentile, age,
or category is source-specific metadata, not the Vitalspan official percentile.

## 6. Reference Dataset Selection

### 6.1 Official primary reference

The **Updated Reference Standards for Cardiorespiratory Fitness Measured with
Cardiopulmonary Exercise Testing: Data from FRIEND**, published in 2022, are selected
as Vitalspan's first official normative reference
([Kaminsky et al.](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0025619621006455);
[PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34809986/)).

The selection applies only to:

- apparently healthy U.S. adults aged 20–89;
- source-recorded male or female strata represented in the publication;
- directly measured treadmill or cycle CPET;
- the matching exercise modality;
- the compatible reported peak/maximal endpoint and effort definition; and
- the exact published age and percentile structure.

The study included **22,379 tests from 34 U.S. laboratories**, with 16,278 treadmill
and 6,101 cycle tests. It includes 12,003 tests in men and 10,376 in women and
publishes sex-specific percentiles by decade for both modalities and two reported
effort criteria. Its strengths are direct gas measurement, large multicentre samples,
broad adult age coverage, explicit modality separation, laboratory screening, and an
actively maintained registry.

Its limitations are equally important: it is a retrospective aggregation of
laboratory and clinical referrals rather than a probability sample of the U.S.;
protocols, carts, averaging periods, and referral patterns vary; race and ethnicity
are not sufficiently characterized for subgroup norms; older-age samples remain
smaller; and observations span several decades. The authors themselves advise
caution outside the United States.

**Selection confidence: High for eligible U.S. direct CPET; insufficient as a
universal global reference.**

### 6.2 International policy

Vitalspan must not deploy a single pooled international percentile distribution.
The strongest current evidence against doing so is the 2026 ERS technical standard,
endorsed by the ERS Executive Committee and ATS. It assembled individual cycle-CPET
data from **5,956 healthy people aged 6–83 across 17 sites** in Europe, North and
South America, and Asia. Geographic region, metabolic-cart type, and peak averaging
method contributed to heterogeneity; important age bands were underrepresented; and
the task force could not generate generalizable Global Lung Function Initiative
reference ranges
([Radtke et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41786497/)).

The 2020 international FRIEND report is valuable evidence of cross-country
differences, not authorization for a universal norm. It combined 11,678 FRIEND-I
maximal treadmill tests from three countries with 32,329 observations from six
countries in published studies, ages 20–79. The paper explicitly described the work
as beginning the process of global standard development and found country differences
([Peterman et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31883698/)).

**Official policy:** one scientific framework, multiple admitted reference families,
and no silent global fallback.

### 7.2 Candidate dossiers

#### A. Updated FRIEND Registry — selected

**Publication:** Kaminsky et al., *Mayo Clinic Proceedings*, 2022.  
**Population:** Apparently healthy adults tested in U.S. clinical and research
laboratories.  
**Sample:** 22,379 tests; 12,003 men, 10,376 women.  
**Age:** 20–89.  
**Ethnicity:** Not adequately reported for ethnicity-specific norms.  
**Country:** United States; multicentre.  
**Method:** Direct gas-exchange CPET; treadmill and cycle kept separate.  
**Laboratory quality:** High registry-level screening, experienced laboratories, and
published-guideline testing; heterogeneous carts, protocols, and averaging remain.  
**Clinical acceptance:** High and growing; developed to replace workload-derived U.S.
norms.  
**External validation:** A related FRIEND prediction model was tested in a separate
1,287-person cohort, but this does not constitute external validation of every
published percentile.  
**Current adoption:** High in U.S. CPET literature and reference work.  
**Strengths:** Largest mature U.S. direct-CPET reference; balanced overall sex
representation; older adults; both modalities; transparent percentile tables.  
**Limitations:** Referral/volunteer sample, not population probability sampling;
method heterogeneity; long collection period; limited demographic subgroup detail;
smallest cells at oldest ages.  
**Disposition:** Official primary reference under exact matching.

#### B. Original FRIEND treadmill and cycle publications — superseded

The 2015 treadmill report included 7,783 adults aged 20–79 from eight U.S.
laboratories and demonstrated why direct measurement improved upon earlier
workload-derived standards
([Kaminsky et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4919021/)). The 2017
cycle report included 4,494 maximal tests from ten laboratories and established
separate cycle norms rather than applying a fixed treadmill adjustment
([Kaminsky et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27938891/)).

Both have high laboratory relevance and strong clinical acceptance. Both are now
superseded for general U.S. adult use by the larger 2022 publication. They must not
remain active as competing default versions.

#### C. ACSM reference standards — guideline, not dataset

**Publication:** *ACSM's Guidelines for Exercise Testing and Prescription*, 12th
edition
([ACSM official publication page](https://acsm.org/education-resources/books/guidelines-exercise-testing-prescription/)).  
**Population/sample/country:** Not one primary cohort; tables and interpretation
guidance depend on the cited underlying source and edition.  
**Method:** Professional synthesis covering direct and estimated exercise testing.  
**Laboratory quality:** Guideline-level authority; underlying dataset quality varies.  
**Clinical acceptance/adoption:** Very high in exercise medicine and exercise
professional practice.  
**External validation:** Must be assessed for each source table, not attributed to
the ACSM book as a whole.  
**Strengths:** Broad professional adoption, practical standards, continuing edition
governance.  
**Limitations:** A textbook table is not a new representative reference cohort;
edition changes and source attribution matter; some widely circulated categories
trace to estimated Cooper norms.  
**Disposition:** Use ACSM for measurement and practice context. Do not label an
ACSM-derived table as an independent normative dataset. The original cited dataset
must pass Vitalspan admission.

#### D. ESC/EAPC and European society evidence — guidance, not one norm

The European Society of Cardiology recognizes CPET and peak oxygen uptake for
exercise assessment, prescription, and risk stratification in selected clinical
populations. The 2020 sports cardiology guideline describes CPET as useful for adult
and older individuals and essential in several disease pathways
([ESC guideline](https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/42/1/17/5898937)).
The European Association of Preventive Cardiology also uses measured peak responses
for individualized exercise prescription
([EAPC position statement](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34077542/)).

These documents do not establish a single pan-European, apparently healthy VO₂max
reference distribution. “European Society values” must therefore not be represented
as one dataset. European normative interpretation must name the actual cohort, such
as HUNT3, LowLands, Prevention First, or SHIP, and match its modality and population.

#### E. Cooper Institute and Cooper Center Longitudinal Study — legacy/context

**Publication/source:** Cooper Institute adult fitness norms and the Cooper Center
Longitudinal Study (CCLS), developed from preventive examinations at the Cooper
Clinic in Dallas
([CCLS overview](https://www.cooperinstitute.org/research/ccls)).  
**Population:** A very large, self-selected preventive-clinic cohort accumulated over
decades; historically predominantly male, White, well educated, and higher
socioeconomic status.  
**Sample/age/sex:** The longitudinal resource exceeds 120,000 participants and
hundreds of thousands of visits, but individual normative tables use specific
subsets and age-sex strata that must not be conflated with the entire cohort.  
**Ethnicity/country:** United States; limited historical transportability to the
diversity of the contemporary population.  
**Method:** Maximal Balke treadmill performance. Widely used adult VO₂max norms were
predicted from workload or test duration, not directly measured respiratory gas
exchange, as documented in the FRIEND comparison.  
**Laboratory quality:** Strong standardized clinical exercise-testing tradition;
lower criterion validity for exact VO₂max norms because of estimation.  
**Clinical acceptance/adoption:** Historically very high; still embedded in manuals
and consumer-device rating tables.  
**External validation:** Exceptional outcome follow-up for mortality and morbidity;
this validates the prognostic value of treadmill fitness, not numerical equivalence
to direct CPET VO₂max.  
**Strengths:** Scale, longitudinal outcomes, age-sex fitness stratification, and
landmark mortality evidence.  
**Limitations:** Estimated oxygen uptake, selected clinic cohort, historical
demographic imbalance, and multiple table versions.  
**Disposition:** Do not use as the official direct-CPET percentile. Retain as outcome
and historical classification evidence.

#### F. HUNT3 Fitness Study — strong Norwegian regional candidate

**Publication:** Loe et al., Norwegian population-based HUNT3 Fitness Study
([full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4245230/)).  
**Population:** Healthy participants recruited from the population-based Trøndelag
Health Study in three Norwegian municipalities.  
**Sample:** 4,631; 2,261 men and 2,370 women.  
**Age:** Approximately 20–90.  
**Ethnicity/country:** Norway; ethnicity was not developed as a transportable
stratifier.  
**Method:** Direct treadmill CPET with an individualized graded protocol.  
**Laboratory quality:** High; standardized procedures, calibration, equipment
validation, and detailed health exclusions.  
**Clinical acceptance/adoption:** High in Norwegian and international reference
comparisons.  
**External validation:** Extensively compared with other cohorts; not a validated
universal external norm.  
**Strengths:** Population base, both sexes, broad age span, direct treadmill gas
measurement, strong quality procedures.  
**Limitations:** Volunteer and healthy-participant selection; three municipalities;
Norwegian activity and health context; some participants reached VO₂peak rather than
a demonstrated plateau. HUNT values are materially higher than U.S. FRIEND values.  
**Disposition:** Eligible for a Norway-specific treadmill reference after a formal
transportability check. Not a pan-European default.

#### G. LowLands Fitness Registry — Dutch/Flemish cycle candidate

**Publication:** van der Steeg and Takken, 2021
([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33521869/)).  
**Population:** Apparently healthy people undergoing professional health checks in
the Netherlands and Flanders.  
**Sample:** 4,612; 3,671 male and 941 female.  
**Age:** 6–65.  
**Ethnicity/country:** Netherlands and Belgium; ethnicity insufficiently reported
for subgroup interpretation.  
**Method:** Direct maximal cycle CPET; continuous centile curves.  
**Laboratory quality:** Moderate to high multicentre quality; source centres and
protocols varied.  
**Clinical acceptance/adoption:** Important West-European regional reference.  
**External validation:** A separate sample was used for cross-validation, but the
female validation sample was small.  
**Strengths:** Current direct data, broad age range including youth, centiles rather
than only means, and modality specificity.  
**Limitations:** Marked male imbalance; professional, military, and health-check
selection; exclusion of smokers and people with obesity; high observed fitness;
limited coverage above 65.  
**Disposition:** Candidate for Dutch/Flemish cycle interpretation in its supported
population; must not be generalized to all Western Europe.

#### H. Prevention First Registry — selected German occupational cohort

**Publication:** Rapp et al., 2018
([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29506981/)).  
**Population:** Adult volunteers receiving preventive health examinations, largely
white-collar employees referred through companies.  
**Sample:** 10,090; 6,462 men and 3,628 women.  
**Age:** 21–83.  
**Ethnicity/country:** Germany; ethnicity not adequately characterized for subgroup
norms.  
**Method:** Direct incremental cycle CPET across three private clinics.  
**Laboratory quality:** Moderate to high; clinically standardized, but effort
verification was examined in only a subsample.  
**Clinical acceptance/adoption:** Recognized European reference dataset.  
**External validation:** Comparisons with FRIEND and SHIP; no broad prospective
international validation.  
**Strengths:** Large adult sample, both sexes, broad age range, direct gas exchange,
age-sex quantiles.  
**Limitations:** Occupational and preventive-clinic selection, sedentary white-collar
emphasis, retrospective aggregation, and limited population representativeness.  
**Disposition:** Regional research candidate, not Germany's automatic national
default without target-population validation.

#### I. SHIP — high phenotyping, smaller healthy reference sample

**Publication:** Koch et al., 2009
([ERS full text](https://publications.ersnet.org/content/erj/33/2/389)).  
**Population:** Population-based Study of Health in Pomerania, northeast Germany,
with extensive cardiopulmonary health screening.  
**Sample:** 1,708 underwent CPET; stringent exclusions produced a healthy reference
sample of 534, including 253 men and 281 women.  
**Age:** 25–80.  
**Ethnicity/country:** Northeast Germany; ethnicity not established as a reference
stratifier.  
**Method:** Standardized progressive cycle CPET.  
**Laboratory quality:** High, including ECG, echocardiography, lung function, medical
history, and controlled health exclusions.  
**Clinical acceptance/adoption:** Important clinical physiology reference; less
widely used than FRIEND.  
**External validation:** Used in comparative literature; limited independent
validation of the final healthy ranges.  
**Strengths:** Population sampling and unusually rigorous healthy phenotype.  
**Limitations:** Small final reference cells, regional sample, and exclusion of
smokers and people with obesity reduces real-population representativeness.  
**Disposition:** Strong scientific comparator and regional research candidate.

#### J. Fleury and pooled Brazilian standards — Brazilian candidates

**Publication:** Rossi Neto et al., 2019
([full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6326491/)); pooled Brazilian
analysis published in 2022
([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35562324/)).  
**Population:** Fleury laboratory participants without structural heart disease;
the later work pooled Brazilian data and examined national and international
heterogeneity.  
**Sample:** 18,189 in the Fleury report.  
**Age:** 7–84, reported in six age groups.  
**Sex:** Men and women with age-sex percentiles; representation varies sharply in
the oldest bands.  
**Ethnicity/country:** Brazil; ethnicity and the country's diversity were not
captured well enough for ethnicity-specific norms.  
**Method:** Direct treadmill CPET.  
**Laboratory quality:** Moderate to high within an experienced laboratory network;
clinical referral and protocol selection remain relevant.  
**Clinical acceptance/adoption:** Significant Brazilian reference resource.  
**External validation:** Comparisons with FRIEND and HUNT; the pooled analysis found
differences even between Brazilian regions.  
**Strengths:** Large direct-CPET sample, wide age span, country-specific data, and
explicit percentile reporting.  
**Limitations:** Laboratory/referral rather than national probability sampling;
“free of structural heart disease” is not identical to rigorously healthy;
geographic, socioeconomic, activity, and oldest-age representation limitations.  
**Disposition:** Candidate for a Brazilian treadmill reference after target-region
validation. The pooled regional differences argue against both a U.S. fallback and
an unqualified Brazil-wide assumption.

## 8. Systematic-review and guideline summary

The 2014 systematic review by Paap and Takken identified 35 CPET reference studies
and concluded that none was an ideal universal source. It advised laboratories to
choose references that resemble the tested population, equipment, protocol, and
method
([Paap and Takken](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25418758/)).

The 2019 update reviewed publications from March 2014 through February 2019. It
included 29 studies and 87,256 participants aged 6–90. Fourteen studies used cycle,
14 treadmill, and one both. Only four studies met the review's high-quality threshold;
none met every quality criterion. Population and methodological heterogeneity made
meta-analysis inappropriate
([Takken et al.](https://doi.org/10.1080/14779072.2019.1627874)).

The 2026 ERS/ATS-endorsed technical standard is the most current international
assessment. Despite individual-level data and modern modelling, it could not produce
generalizable global ranges. This is decisive evidence that Vitalspan should not
manufacture a pooled global norm from the presently available literature.

Guidelines converge on three principles:

- reference selection is clinically important;
- the selected population and test method must match the person and CPET protocol;
  and
- currently available references remain heterogeneous.

The AHA's scientific statement establishes CRF as a clinically important vital sign,
but it also identifies a need for better age-, sex-, and race/ethnicity-aware cut
points
([AHA statement](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27881567/)). Recognition of CRF's
importance does not create a universal classification table.

## 9. Normative Population Policy

### 9.1 Required grouping

Vitalspan must use **age plus source-recorded sex plus region/reference population
plus modality**. These variables serve different scientific purposes:

- **Age:** VO₂peak distributions decline substantially across adulthood. Age-only
  interpretation is insufficient because sex and modality differences remain.
- **Sex:** Published adult reference distributions consistently differ between the
  male and female strata. Sex-only interpretation is insufficient because age and
  modality effects remain.
- **Region/reference population:** Cross-country and within-country differences are
  documented. Region determines transportability; it must not be presented as an
  innate biological correction.
- **Modality:** Treadmill and cycle values differ systematically and cannot share one
  percentile table.
- **Endpoint and effort definition:** VO₂max, VO₂peak, and differing effort rules
  must not be silently mixed.
- **Health population:** Apparently healthy and disease-specific distributions answer
  different questions.

### 9.2 Age policy

Vitalspan must follow the age handling published by the admitted reference:

- If a source publishes decade bands, use those bands exactly.
- If a source publishes validated continuous centiles, use only that published
  continuous method and supported age range.
- Do not interpolate between categorical bands, extrapolate beyond the oldest or
  youngest supported age, or transfer an adult distribution to a child.
- Age at the test timestamp, not current age, is the relevant reference age.
- A historical measurement retains its original reference result and reference
  version; it must not drift as the person ages.

### 9.3 Sex and gender policy

Most reviewed reference datasets publish binary male and female strata and do not
adequately describe how sex was recorded. Vitalspan must therefore:

- describe the matching field as **source-recorded sex used by the reference**, not
  gender identity;
- never infer sex from name, appearance, wearable profile, or other proxy;
- preserve self-described gender separately when available;
- not force an intersex, unknown, undisclosed, or otherwise unrepresented person
  into a reference stratum; and
- return “reference not established for the available sex data” when a scientifically
  supported match is absent.

For transgender people, hormones, body composition, life stage, and the purpose of
assessment can materially affect physiology, while the reviewed normative cohorts do
not provide an adequately validated universal matching rule. No automatic reassignment
is authorized. A clinician may interpret the complete CPET in context, but Vitalspan
must not invent a normative mapping.

### 9.4 Region, country, and ethnicity policy

Vitalspan should select the narrowest admitted reference that is demonstrably
transportable to the target service population. Country or region is a proxy for the
study's sampling frame, physical-activity culture, body-size distribution, health
selection, socioeconomic conditions, equipment, and testing practice. It is not an
assertion that national groups have fixed biological capacities.

Ethnicity must not be used as an automatic adjustment in the current standard because:

- many datasets do not report it adequately;
- subgroup sample sizes and external validation are insufficient;
- ethnicity categories differ between countries and over time;
- observed differences can reflect social, environmental, and sampling effects; and
- an adjustment could encode inequity while creating false precision.

Ethnicity and race should be retained, with consent and lawful governance, for
representativeness analysis, calibration monitoring, and fairness audits. A future
ethnicity-aware reference requires a prespecified physiological rationale, adequate
subgroup data, independent validation, and an equity review.

### 9.5 Body size and activity

Body-mass-normalized oxygen uptake is not neutral. Adiposity can lower a value per
unit of total body mass without an equivalent reduction in absolute oxygen transport,
and training status strongly shifts the distribution. The current official reference
must preserve the exact published expression and population rules.

Vitalspan must not add BMI, lean mass, athlete status, or activity-level corrections
unless an admitted reference explicitly validates them. Recent FRIEND body-composition
work is scientifically promising but does not yet replace the 2022 official percentile
standard
([FRIEND body-composition study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39920345/)).

Elite athletes, children, pregnancy, disability-specific populations, and major
disease groups require their own validated references or no percentile.

## 10. Percentile Standard

### 10.1 Official output

Vitalspan should expose a percentile only when all eligibility and matching rules are
met. The interpretation must be stated as a position within a named cohort, for
example in prose: “relative to the matching age, sex, modality, and regional reference
population.”

The output must also carry:

- reference name and version;
- population and geographic scope;
- age and sex stratum;
- modality and endpoint;
- measured-versus-estimated status;
- reference eligibility status; and
- a limitation statement that percentile is not clinical risk.

### 10.2 Advantages

Percentiles:

- preserve more information than broad categories;
- make the reference population explicit;
- avoid implying a universal disease boundary;
- support comparison without attaching a value judgment to the person;
- reveal distributional position even when conventional labels differ; and
- can be versioned and audited.

### 10.3 Limitations

Percentiles:

- are population dependent and can change with reference selection;
- inherit sampling and measurement bias from the source;
- are not equal intervals of physiological or clinical difference;
- can exaggerate the meaning of small measurement differences near dense parts of a
  distribution;
- do not establish causality, diagnosis, or mortality probability; and
- should not be compared across incompatible references as though identical.

### 10.4 Production choice

**Expose percentiles, not qualitative categories.** If a percentile is scientifically
unsupported, expose neither. The absence of a percentile is preferable to a precise
but invalid global fallback.

## 11. Classification Registry

No reviewed qualitative category system has evidence strong enough to become the
universal Vitalspan classification standard. The strongest supported system is the
published, source-bound percentile itself. Where studies use categories, their
boundaries normally differ by age and sex and sometimes by modality; that dependence
must be preserved rather than averaged away.

| Classification system | Basis | Evidence judgment | Production status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Vitalspan percentile | Exact position in an admitted age-, sex-, region-, modality-, endpoint-, and version-matched distribution | Best supported normative representation | **Official** |
| FRIEND published percentiles | Direct U.S. CPET distributions by age, sex, and modality | High for eligible U.S. adults | **Official source** |
| Very Poor / Poor / Fair / Good / Excellent / Superior | Variable percentile bands, often reproduced from ACSM/Cooper or test-specific manuals | Familiar but not universal, diagnostic, or outcome-derived | **Not authorized** |
| Cooper low / moderate / high | Commonly age-sex cohort quintile groupings from estimated treadmill capacity | Strong cohort outcome evidence; source-specific and estimated | **Context only** |
| Study-specific age-sex quartiles or quintiles | Relative groups created for a cohort analysis | Useful for epidemiology; boundaries change with cohort | **Research only** |
| Mandsager low through elite groups | Age-sex percentiles in 122,007 clinically referred treadmill patients using estimated capacity | Strong mortality gradient; referral population and study-defined bands | **Research/outcome context only** |
| Fixed percent predicted | Ratio to a prediction with a conventional threshold | A fixed value is not a statistically valid lower limit of normal | **Not authorized as the normative standard** |
| Disease-specific clinical threshold | Outcome-validated threshold in a defined disease pathway | May be clinically valid in that exact population and full assessment | **Outside general normative interpretation** |

### 11.1 Why qualitative labels are rejected

The requested labels do not have one stable scientific meaning. Published systems
use different numbers of groups, different percentile boundaries, different age
bands, and direct or estimated methods. Cooper studies commonly define low fitness
as the lowest age-sex quintile and combine other quintiles into moderate and high
groups. Other studies use tertiles, quartiles, or different percentile bands. A large
clinical cohort used five age-sex percentile groups, including an “elite” extreme,
for mortality analysis
([Mandsager et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6324439/)).

These groupings can demonstrate a risk gradient without proving that the boundary is
a biological threshold. Qualitative labels also create cliff effects, can stigmatize,
and conceal the reference population. Vitalspan will not relabel percentiles as
clinical states.

### 11.2 Boundary policy

If a future use case requires categories, it must undergo a separate scientific
phase. Any admitted category must:

- reproduce a named publication exactly;
- retain its source, version, population, method, and purpose;
- avoid clinical or moral language unless clinically validated;
- never be applied outside the publication's supported range; and
- not be described as a mortality threshold unless prospectively validated for that
  purpose.

No local boundary smoothing, interpolation, renamed label, or custom Vitalspan class
is authorized by this standard.

## 12. Mortality, healthy aging, and hospitalization

### 12.1 What the evidence supports

Higher CRF has a strong, graded, and independent association with lower all-cause and
cardiovascular mortality. The landmark meta-analysis of 33 cohorts found graded risk
across harmonized low, intermediate, and high fitness bands
([Kodama et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19454641/)). A 2024 overview of 26
systematic reviews, more than 20.9 million observations, and 199 unique cohorts found
consistent associations with lower mortality and incident chronic disease, while
grading the largely observational evidence from very low to moderate certainty
([Lang et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/)).

The AHA describes substantially elevated risk at very low exercise capacity and
greater survival at higher capacity, with a large share of benefit occurring between
the least-fit group and the next group. These are useful public-health observations,
not an age-, sex-, method-, and population-neutral VO₂max diagnostic cutoff
([AHA summary](https://professional.heart.org/en/science-news/importance-of-assessing-cardiorespiratory-fitness-in-clinical-practice-a-case-for-fitness/top-things-to-know)).

Large clinical treadmill cohorts and veteran cohorts show no clear upper point at
which higher fitness becomes associated with higher mortality. Their capacity values
were commonly estimated from treadmill performance, and their fitness groups were
study-specific. They validate the graded prognostic construct, not direct-CPET
percentile interchangeability.

### 12.2 Why no general mortality threshold is authorized

Published thresholds differ because studies vary in:

- age, sex, race/ethnicity, disease burden, and referral population;
- direct gas measurement versus workload or non-exercise estimation;
- treadmill versus cycle testing;
- absolute, mass-normalized, or exercise-capacity expression;
- choice of cohort quantiles versus prespecified bands;
- follow-up period, outcome definition, and adjustment set; and
- whether the objective was population description, risk stratification, or clinical
  decision-making.

Risk is generally continuous. A percentile boundary is not proof of a sudden change
in biology. Vitalspan must not translate a VO₂max value or percentile into mortality
probability, years of life, “longevity age,” or safety reassurance.

### 12.3 Sex and age

Absolute values associated with a given outcome will differ across age and sex because
both fitness distributions and baseline risk differ. Some cohort studies reduce this
problem by using age-sex-specific quantiles, but those quantiles remain cohort-relative.
There is no validated universal threshold that can be applied unchanged across adult
age and sex strata.

### 12.4 Healthy aging

Higher midlife CRF is associated with lower later-life chronic disease burden,
heart-failure hospitalization, disability, and mortality in observational cohorts.
Exercise training can improve CRF across adult age. These findings support preserving
longitudinal measured fitness as a healthy-aging domain.

They do not define one VO₂max threshold for “healthy aging.” Functional independence
depends on task demands, strength, balance, cognition, disease, body composition, and
environment as well as aerobic capacity. Vitalspan must not label a person as aging
healthily or unhealthily from VO₂max alone.

### 12.5 Hospitalization

Cooper longitudinal analyses found lower later-life heart-failure hospitalization
among people with higher age-sex-specific midlife treadmill fitness. Other large
observational cohorts show lower admission rates when estimated CRF is maintained or
increased. These studies use cohort groups and estimated capacity and do not establish
a universal admission threshold
([Cooper HF study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5152948/)).

No all-cause hospitalization cutoff is authorized for general Vitalspan production.

### 12.6 Disease-specific clinical thresholds

Numerical peak-oxygen thresholds are used in defined clinical pathways, particularly
advanced heart failure and transplant evaluation. Their validity depends on diagnosis,
medication status, symptom-limited direct CPET, effort, complementary CPET variables,
and specialist interpretation. Current heart-transplant guidance illustrates this
context-specific use
([ISHLT guideline](https://www.ishlt.org/education-and-publications/standards-guidelines-detail/ishlt-guidelines-for-the-evaluation-and-care-of-cardiac-transplant-candidates)).

Such thresholds must never be imported into the apparently healthy normative standard
or applied to consumer estimates. They belong to a separate disease-specific clinical
module with its own governance.

## 13. Interpretation Standard

### 13.1 Authorized interpretation

A production normative statement is authorized only when:

1. the measurement is eligible under Phase 5.0B;
2. the reference is active and versioned;
3. age, source-recorded sex, region, modality, endpoint, unit expression, and health
   population match;
4. the person lies inside every supported range;
5. the percentile comes directly from the published reference structure; and
6. the output preserves reference and measurement provenance.

The statement may describe relative position within the reference distribution. It
must not diagnose low fitness, promise longevity, prescribe exercise, or claim a
clinical risk category.

### 13.2 Unsupported interpretation

Vitalspan must return a scientifically explicit unsupported state when:

- country or region has no admitted reference;
- age is outside the source range;
- sex data cannot be matched without inference;
- modality is missing or mismatched;
- the value is a wearable, field, workload, or non-exercise estimate;
- VO₂peak/VO₂max compatibility cannot be established;
- the reference is disease-specific but the person is not in that pathway;
- the result unit or body-size expression is incompatible; or
- provenance or test quality is insufficient.

“No supported reference” means evidence is unavailable; it does not mean the
measurement is poor or the person is abnormal.

### 13.3 Longitudinal interpretation

Historical percentiles must remain attached to the reference version originally used.
When a reference is updated, Vitalspan may produce a separately versioned
reinterpretation for scientific comparison, but must not overwrite the original.

Longitudinal change should preferentially use the same modality, protocol, endpoint,
laboratory class, and reference family. A change in percentile can reflect a change in
fitness, age band, reference version, body mass, test method, or measurement noise.
It must not be described as physiological change without examining those factors.

## 14. Reference Governance

### 14.1 Reference admission dossier

Every new reference must have an approved dossier containing:

- full citation, persistent identifier, and publication status;
- dataset owner and licensing status;
- country, sites, recruitment frame, and collection years;
- sample size by age and source-recorded sex;
- race/ethnicity reporting and representativeness gaps;
- health inclusion and exclusion criteria;
- treadmill/cycle modality, protocol, endpoint, and effort definition;
- equipment, calibration, averaging, and quality-control procedures;
- unit and body-size expression;
- percentile or centile derivation method as published;
- internal and external validation;
- missingness, exclusions, and smallest-cell review;
- target population and prohibited extrapolations;
- comparison with the current reference;
- fairness and transportability assessment;
- clinical and scientific owner; and
- immutable Vitalspan version, effective date, and review date.

### 14.2 Activation requirements

A regional reference may become active only when:

- direct CPET and measurement definitions meet Phase 5.0B;
- the population reasonably represents the target service population;
- the exact modality and endpoint are supported;
- both statistical and clinical transportability have been evaluated;
- subgroup performance and unsupported groups are documented;
- source rights permit the intended use;
- an independent scientific reviewer approves the dossier; and
- the prior-reference migration and audit plan is complete.

Large sample size alone is insufficient. A smaller, well-characterized population may
be scientifically preferable to a larger selected clinical sample for a particular
region.

### 14.3 Versioning and auditability

Every result must retain:

- the raw measurement and its measurement provenance;
- the reference identifier and version;
- the exact source stratum used;
- date of interpretation;
- eligibility and unsupported-reason status;
- source publication and any licensed table version; and
- correction, reinterpretation, and retirement history.

Reference data must never be silently edited. Corrections require a new immutable
version and a documented impact review.

### 14.4 Monitoring

Active references should be monitored at least annually for:

- new society standards and systematic reviews;
- corrected or retracted source publications;
- registry updates and improved age or demographic coverage;
- calibration drift in the Vitalspan target population;
- missing-reference rates by country and demographic group;
- systematic percentile differences by laboratory or device pathway;
- evidence of subgroup miscalibration or inequity; and
- licensing or provenance changes.

Monitoring must not be used to tune cut points informally. Any scientific change
requires a governed new version.

### 14.5 Retirement

A reference should be retired when it is superseded by a demonstrably stronger source,
its methods become incompatible with accepted measurement standards, material bias is
identified, the publication is corrected or withdrawn, or continuing use is no longer
legally or scientifically supportable.

Retirement stops new use but does not erase historical interpretations. Historical
records retain the old version and retirement notice.

## 15. Future Update Policy

Priority updates are:

1. prospective international direct-CPET collection with standardized carts,
   protocols, averaging, quality assurance, and centralized over-reading;
2. true population sampling rather than only clinical or occupational volunteers;
3. adequate representation at older ages and across sex, gender-relevant physiology,
   disability, body composition, and underrepresented populations;
4. external validation of percentile calibration, not only prediction averages;
5. paired treadmill and cycle evidence where modality transfer is investigated;
6. separate pediatric, pregnancy, athlete, and disease-specific reference families;
7. transparent investigation of social, geographic, and ethnic heterogeneity without
   assuming biological essentialism; and
8. validation of whether outcome-linked interpretations add value beyond normative
   percentile while avoiding risk overstatement.

The planned GLI-style international work described by ERS is the most important
future candidate. Vitalspan should reassess the global-policy decision when a
prospectively standardized, adequately representative, externally validated all-age
reference is published. Until then, “global” must not mean “pooled from whatever data
are available.”

## 17. Production policy

### Accepted

- Exact-match percentile interpretation using the active 2022 FRIEND reference for
  eligible U.S. adult direct treadmill or cycle CPET.
- Separately governed regional references after full admission.
- An explicit unsupported state when no valid reference exists.
- Historical retention of raw measurements and versioned interpretations.

### Rejected

- One global default reference.
- Silent fallback to FRIEND outside its target population.
- Mixing treadmill and cycle distributions.
- Applying direct-CPET norms to wearable or other estimated values.
- Unversioned “ACSM,” “European,” or “Cooper” labels without the actual source.
- Automatic ethnicity correction or inferred sex.
- Extrapolation beyond published age or population ranges.
- Official “Very Poor” through “Superior” categories.
- Mortality, longevity, hospitalization, or healthy-aging claims from a percentile.
- Disease-specific thresholds in the general normative pathway.
- Custom Vitalspan boundaries, equations, scores, or fitness ages.

## 18. Final Scientific Recommendation

### Final production recommendation: Production Ready — conditional

The VO₂max Reference Standard is ready for production as a **strictly governed,
reference-bound percentile interpretation layer**.

Production readiness applies now to eligible U.S. adult direct CPET using the 2022
FRIEND reference and to future regional references that complete the admission
process. It does not apply to universal global interpretation, qualitative fitness
labels, wearable-reference interchangeability, or mortality thresholds.

The scientific principle is simple: **use the best matching published reference, name
it, version it, preserve its limits, and return no percentile when the match is not
supported.**
